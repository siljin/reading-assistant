Towards Conversational Diagnostic AI

Tao Tu (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Anil Palepu (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Mike Schaekermann (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Khaled Saab (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Ryutaro Tanno (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Karan Singhal (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Alan Karthikesalingam (Google Research / Google DeepMind), Vivek Natarajan (Google Research / Google DeepMind)

arXiv (later Nature, 2025) · 2024

Paper ↗

TL;DR

An LLM trained by talking to itself (simulated doctor + simulated patient) out-scored real primary-care physicians on diagnostic accuracy and most bedside-manner axes in a blinded, text-only OSCE study.

No background needed

Explain it simply

Think of it like a medical student who practices by role-playing both the doctor AND the patient, millions of times, grading every conversation, until the doctor half gets very good.

AMIE is a chatbot built to take a patient's history and reach a diagnosis through conversation. To get good without endless real patients, it practices in a simulator where one copy plays the patient and another plays the doctor, and an automatic grader gives feedback. When tested against 20 real doctors over text chats, specialists rated AMIE higher on diagnosis and on most communication qualities.

Plain-language summary

Good diagnosis starts with a good conversation: asking the right questions, in the right order, with empathy. The authors built AMIE, an LLM tuned specifically for that diagnostic dialogue. The trick is self-play: instead of needing huge volumes of real doctor-patient transcripts, AMIE practices inside a simulated clinic — one model instance acts as the patient, another as the doctor, and an automated critic scores each exchange and feeds back corrections. This lets training scale across many diseases and contexts cheaply. They then ran a randomized, double-blind crossover OSCE (a standardized clinical exam format) with validated patient-actors across 149 scenarios from Canada, the UK and India, pitting AMIE against 20 primary-care physicians over text chat. Specialists scored AMIE ahead on 28 of 32 axes; patient-actors on 24 of 26. The catch: it's text-only, the doctors were boxed into an unfamiliar chat interface, and 'beats doctors on a chat exam' is not 'safe to deploy in a real clinic.'

What's actually new

Methodologically novel
Before

Medical LLMs were tuned on static QA benchmarks (e.g. USMLE-style), which don't test the live, multi-turn skill of taking a history through conversation.

After

A self-play simulated-clinic training loop + a multi-axis clinical eval, with a blinded head-to-head against real PCPs showing the system can lead on diagnosis and communication in a text OSCE.

Methods — what they actually did

  • Base: an LLM further tuned for diagnostic dialogue.
  • Self-play loop: a simulated environment where the model role-plays patient and doctor; an automated critic scores exchanges and feeds back, letting training scale across diseases/specialties without proportional real data.
  • Inference: a chain-of-reasoning strategy to structure history-taking and differential diagnosis over turns.
  • Evaluation: randomized, double-blind crossover OSCE; 149 scenarios (Canada/UK/India); 20 PCPs as baseline; validated patient-actors; rated by specialists and the actors on 30+ clinically meaningful axes.

Results — and what they support

AMIE led on diagnostic accuracy vs PCPs.

Evidence Top differential diagnosis accuracy higher under specialist rating.

Caveat Text-chat only; PCPs may underperform vs their normal in-person workflow.

AMIE rated higher on 28 of 32 axes by specialists.

Evidence Blinded specialist scoring across history-taking, management, communication, empathy.

Caveat Axes are correlated; '28 of 32' is not 28 independent wins.

AMIE rated higher on 24 of 26 axes by patient-actors.

Evidence Patient-actor post-consult ratings.

Caveat Actors, not real patients; novelty/verbosity of an AI may inflate perceived empathy.

Limitations

Stated by authors

  • Text-only chat, not multimodal or real clinical setting.
  • Patient-actors and OSCE scenarios, not real patients.
  • Not a deployment-ready or regulated system.

Unstated

  • PCPs were constrained to an unfamiliar text interface — the human baseline may be artificially weak.
  • No real safety/harm outcome measured; 'preferred on chat' ≠ 'safe to act on.'
  • Self-play critic defines 'good' — risk of optimizing to the grader rather than to real clinical correctness.
  • Geographic/scenario coverage is curated; tail and rare presentations untested.
  • Empathy ratings can reward fluent verbosity, which an LLM produces by default.

Could you actually use this?

Effort: research project

What you'd need

  • Frontier LLM access + heavy tuning compute
  • A simulated-patient environment and an automated clinical critic
  • Clinically validated eval scenarios and rater panels (expensive, slow)
  • Regulatory/ethics pathway for anything beyond research

Cheapest validation

Reproduce just the self-play loop on one specialty with an open LLM and a simple rubric critic; measure whether simulated history-taking quality improves over turns before touching any human eval.

So what?

For research

  • Self-play for dialogue skills (not just games/math) is viable — expect it applied to other expert-conversation domains.
  • Pushes eval away from static QA toward multi-turn, multi-axis clinical assessment.

For practice

  • Don't read this as 'AI beats doctors' — read it as 'structured AI history-taking is now strong enough to prototype triage/intake tools' under heavy human oversight.
  • The constrained-interface caveat matters: any product comparison must match the human's real workflow, not a chat box.
